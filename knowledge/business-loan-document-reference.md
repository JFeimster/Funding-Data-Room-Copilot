# Business Funding Document Reference

## How to Use This Reference

This guide explains common document categories that may appear in a business funding file. Requirements vary by provider, product, business type, and applicant profile.

The Funding Data Room Copilot should use this reference to explain documents in plain English—not to declare that every file is mandatory.

## Business Identity Documents

### What They Are

Records that establish the business’s legal identity, such as formation documents, EIN records, licenses, and address information.

### Why They May Be Requested

They may help confirm the entity, operating history, location, and authority to transact.

### Common Problems

- legal name differs across documents
- old address
- inactive or expired license
- DBA not linked to the legal entity
- incomplete formation documents

### Who Normally Provides Them

The owner, registered agent, attorney, state filing office, or internal records administrator.

### Human Review

Required when names, dates, ownership, or addresses conflict.

## Ownership Documents

### What They Are

Operating agreements, bylaws, ownership schedules, authorized-signer records, and related documents.

### Why They May Be Requested

They may help identify owners and determine who is authorized to act for the business.

### Common Problems

- ownership percentages do not total correctly
- authorized signer is unclear
- records conflict
- outdated ownership schedule
- sensitive personal data is exposed

### Who Normally Provides Them

The owner, attorney, corporate secretary, or business administrator.

### Human Review

Always required for conflicting ownership or authority.

## Bank Statements

### What They Are

Monthly records from the business’s bank account showing deposits, withdrawals, balances, and account activity.

### Why They May Be Requested

They may help a reviewer understand cash-flow activity, account consistency, and recent operations.

### Common Problems

- screenshots instead of full statements
- missing pages
- missing months
- personal account submitted instead of business account
- unreadable scans
- unexplained transfers
- file names without month or bank

### Who Normally Provides Them

The business owner or authorized account user.

### Human Review

Required before sharing and when sensitive identifiers remain visible.

## Profit and Loss Statement

### What It Is

A report showing revenue, expenses, and profit or loss over a defined period.

### Why It May Be Requested

It may help explain business performance and current operating activity.

### Common Problems

- no date range
- old report
- estimated figures not labeled
- totals do not reconcile to the accounting source
- cash and accrual methods are confused
- personal expenses included

### Who Normally Prepares It

The bookkeeper, CPA, accounting software, controller, or owner.

### Human Review

Required, especially for manually prepared or estimated reports.

## Balance Sheet

### What It Is

A snapshot of assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific date.

### Why It May Be Requested

It may help explain what the business owns, owes, and has accumulated.

### Common Problems

- report date missing
- negative balances not understood
- duplicate or outdated liabilities
- owner loans not identified
- balance sheet does not balance

### Who Normally Prepares It

The accounting system, bookkeeper, CPA, or controller.

### Human Review

Required before relying on it for a funding package.

## Business Tax Returns

### What They Are

Filed tax returns for the business or business-related schedules.

### Why They May Be Requested

They may provide historical financial and entity information.

### Common Problems

- wrong entity
- incomplete return
- draft labeled as filed
- sensitive identifiers exposed
- return year not clear
- amendment or extension not noted

### Who Normally Provides Them

The owner, CPA, tax preparer, or authorized tax system.

### Human Review

Always required. The Copilot should not interpret tax liability or give tax advice.

## Merchant Processing Statements

### What They Are

Reports from a card processor or payment platform showing transaction volume, fees, chargebacks, reserves, or deposits.

### Why They May Be Requested

They may help explain card-based revenue and payout timing.

### Common Problems

- only one page provided
- processing account does not match the business
- gross sales confused with net deposits
- chargebacks or reserves not explained
- date range incomplete

### Who Normally Provides Them

The owner, processor portal, finance team, or bookkeeper.

### Human Review

Required when summaries are used to explain revenue.

## Accounts Receivable Aging

### What It Is

A report listing unpaid customer invoices by how long they have been outstanding.

### Why It May Be Requested

It may help explain cash tied up in receivables and expected collections.

### Common Problems

- old report
- disputed invoices included without notes
- customer concentration not explained
- totals do not match the accounting system
- invoices unlikely to be collected treated as current

### Who Normally Prepares It

The accounting system, bookkeeper, controller, or accounts receivable team.

### Human Review

Required to confirm collectability and current status.

## Accounts Payable Report

### What It Is

A report showing amounts the business owes vendors and suppliers.

### Why It May Be Requested

It may help explain near-term obligations and working-capital pressure.

### Common Problems

- report is outdated
- duplicate bills
- disputed balances
- overdue obligations not explained
- owner-paid expenses mixed in

### Who Normally Prepares It

The accounting system, bookkeeper, controller, or accounts payable team.

### Human Review

Required before external use.

## Debt Schedule

### What It Is

A structured list of loans, lines, cards, advances, equipment obligations, balances, and payment terms.

### Why It May Be Requested

It may help a reviewer understand existing obligations and payment load.

### Common Problems

- omitted obligations
- outdated balances
- original amount confused with current balance
- frequency not stated
- payoff status unclear
- daily or weekly payment not identified

### Who Normally Prepares It

The owner, bookkeeper, broker, controller, or processor.

### Human Review

Required. Do not infer missing obligations.

## Payroll Reports

### What They Are

Reports showing payroll expense, pay periods, employee counts, taxes, or contractor payments.

### Why They May Be Requested

They may help explain operating expenses and payroll-related funding needs.

### Common Problems

- sensitive employee data exposed
- report period unclear
- contractors mixed with employees
- gross payroll confused with net payroll
- payroll taxes omitted

### Who Normally Provides Them

The payroll provider, bookkeeper, HR, controller, or owner.

### Human Review

Always required due to sensitive data.

## Platform Payout Reports

### What They Are

Reports from ecommerce, marketplace, or payment platforms showing sales, fees, reserves, and payouts.

### Why They May Be Requested

They may help connect sales activity to bank deposits and payout timing.

### Common Problems

- sales confused with cash received
- payout holds not noted
- returns or chargebacks omitted
- multiple stores combined without explanation
- report period does not match bank statements

### Who Normally Provides Them

The platform administrator, owner, ecommerce operator, or bookkeeper.

### Human Review

Required when used to explain cash flow.

## Use-of-Funds Statement

### What It Is

A short factual explanation of how the requested capital is intended to be used.

### Why It May Be Requested

It helps make the request specific and operational.

### Common Problems

- vague wording such as “growth”
- amount does not match the request
- multiple uses with no allocation
- unsupported projections
- funding purpose changes across forms

### Who Normally Prepares It

The business owner, with support from a broker, advisor, or finance professional.

### Human Review

Required before submission.

### Example

```text
The business is organizing a request for $75,000. The intended use is approximately $45,000 for inventory purchases, $20,000 for payroll and operating reserves during the inventory-to-payout cycle, and $10,000 for freight and supplier deposits.
```

## Supporting Context Documents

These may include:

- equipment quotes
- purchase orders
- customer contracts
- invoices
- backlog reports
- lease agreements
- sales reports
- processor reserve notices
- insurance reimbursement reports
- supplier terms
- written explanations of one-time events

They should support factual statements. They should not be created to manufacture a better story.

## Common Document Quality Problems

| Problem | Suggested Priority |
|---|---|
| Missing requested statement month | Blocker |
| Missing statement pages | Blocker |
| Conflicting legal business names | Blocker |
| Sensitive ID exposed in working notes | Blocker |
| Outdated P&L | Fix Before Submission |
| Unexplained large transfer | Fix Before Submission |
| Inconsistent file names | Improve If Time Allows or Fix Before Submission |
| Optional context missing | Improve If Time Allows |

Priority is operational and does not predict approval.

## Requirements-Vary Disclaimer

Document requirements vary. The Copilot should phrase recommendations as:

- “may be requested”
- “commonly used”
- “confirm with the provider or human reviewer”
- “requirements vary by product and profile”

> Final review still needs a human. Clean organization helps the file, but it does not guarantee approval.
